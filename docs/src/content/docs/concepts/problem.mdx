## TL;DR

**A markdown knowledge base is great in private. Turning it into a *contributable* public wiki, without flattening its rich syntax or forcing contributors to learn git, is a problem nobody has fully solved.** That is what cyberbaser is for: an open layer that lets any markdown knowledge base be authored from many tools, contributed to by anyone on your terms, and published anywhere, while it stays plain files you own. (Obsidian is the first-class example; the `cyberbase` vault is just our first dogfood content, not the scope.)

## The specific pain

A knowledge owner — a researcher, a team, a community, a docs maintainer — wants to publish a body of knowledge, both for themselves and for others to find, reference, and correct. They already use Obsidian (or something like it) as their authoring surface. They reach for a publisher and find three kinds of tools:

1. **Obsidian-aware publishers** (Obsidian Publish, Quartz, Digital Garden). Great fidelity — wikilinks work, callouts render, embeds show up. But contribution is an afterthought. Readers can't edit. At best, you get a GitHub "Edit" link that drops them into a raw markdown editor with no Obsidian syntax support.
2. **Git-backed CMSes** (Decap, Sveltia, Tina). Great contribution story — Open Authoring lets anonymous readers submit PRs. But they don't understand Obsidian syntax. Wikilinks flatten to plain text. Callouts become quoted lines. The vault-side user and the CMS user are writing different dialects.
3. **Docs platforms repurposed as wikis** (Docusaurus, Mintlify, GitBook). Beautiful reader UX, polished contribution flows. But opinionated content schemas; forcing Obsidian content into their shape is lossy and maintenance-heavy.

None of these occupy the interior of the triangle. **Cyberbaser's hypothesis is that the gap is a translation-layer problem, not a new-SSG problem** — if you can guarantee round-trip fidelity between Obsidian syntax and a web-edit surface, you can compose an Obsidian-aware publisher with a git-backed CMS and get all three properties.

## Who it's for

Concrete situations, not personas:

- **You keep a knowledge base in markdown** (usually Obsidian, but any markdown tool) and want to share it publicly, without hand-running a separate publishing stack and without moving into a walled garden like Notion or a paid host.
- **A reader spots a wrong fact** and knows the right one. They don't know git and may not want an account. They'll fix it if it takes a few clicks on the page; they'll walk away if it needs a CLI or a signup.
- **A developer wants to fix a typo** the way they fix anything: edit a `.md` file, open a pull request. They shouldn't have to learn anyone's markdown dialect to do it.
- **An AI agent maintains content**: watching a feed, reconciling sources, drafting updates, and proposing them through the same reviewed pipeline as a human. The tool is built to be agent-friendly, not just human-friendly.

Where the value concentrates: making a knowledge base [**contributable** by anyone on your terms](#contribution-on-your-terms), **mapping cleanly into the CMS and authoring tools people already use**, and **working well with agents**, with Obsidian as a first-class citizen.

Three constraints fall out of the principles. Each existing solution fails at least one.

## Contribution on your terms

The deepest part of cyberbaser isn't the publisher, or even the translation layer. It's *how contribution is governed*. The web is adversarial, so the usual reflex is to demand identity up front (an account, an email, KYC) to keep bad actors out. That reflex is mostly wrong here, for two reasons:

- **Identity gates are mostly theater.** Requiring an email to "reduce abuse" doesn't work; email aliasing is trivial. A GitHub-account requirement mainly filters *out the person you want* (the domain expert who spotted the error) while barely slowing a determined adversary. Friction on the front door buys very little safety.
- **The real question isn't "who are you?" but "can this hurt anything before a human approves it?"** If every contribution lands in a **moderation queue** and nothing goes live until it's reviewed, an anonymous edit *cannot* hurt the content. Moderation, not identity, is the load-bearing safety mechanism.

So the model is simple: **the maintainer controls the trust curve.** Like [Discourse's trust levels](https://blog.discourse.org/2018/06/understanding-discourse-trust-levels/), a contributor earns capability as they prove themselves, and you choose how steep that curve is and what each level requires:

Same content, different governance, set by whoever runs it. **Denial-of-service is a separate problem.** Content abuse is caught by the moderation queue; *volumetric* abuse (DoS, spam floods) is handled at the infrastructure edge (rate-limiting, WAF, see [RA-01](/cyberbaser/design/reference-architectures/self-hosted-forgejo-auth/)). Don't conflate the two.

**Multiple surfaces, one pipeline.** A contributor should be able to act *from wherever they are, however suits them* — and the translation layer is what makes the richest of these possible:

- **In-page, what-you-see-is-what-you-get** — click edit on the rendered page and change it right there. This works *only because* the [translation layer](/cyberbaser/design/translation-layer/) round-trips the edit back to clean Obsidian markdown. (This is why the [round-trip keystone](/cyberbaser/research/v1-architecture/#blockmarkdown-fidelity--the-keystone-rd) is an *enabler* of contribution, not a vanity feature.)
- **Web CMS** form/editor · **Obsidian + git** · **direct GitHub** — for those who prefer them.

Every surface feeds the *same* maintainer-configured pipeline: contribution → (whatever identity/trust step the maintainer set) → moderation queue → merge → live. **Compose, don't build from scratch:** trust levels, identity, moderation queues, and rate-limiting all have mature implementations ([Discourse](https://www.discourse.org/)-style trust, moderation tooling, serverless contribution bots like [Contribunator](https://github.com/Contribunator/Contribunator) / [Staticman](https://staticman.net/), edge WAF). Cyberbaser's job is to *wire them into a coherent pipeline* — a tech-stack design problem, not a from-scratch build. This is the direct answer to the two biggest [product risks](/cyberbaser/research/assumptions-and-risks/) (the account wall, and moderation).
